Do weekdays matter? (χ² test)

For each draw day, we test whether the digit distribution in winning numbers differs from uniform (the fair-lottery hypothesis). A p-value above 0.05 means no evidence of weekday bias.

What are χ² and p-value in plain English?

Roll a die 600 times. You expect each face about 100 times, but you'll actually get something like 96, 104, 99, 107, 95, 99 — never exactly even. χ² (chi-square) is a "surprise score": it adds up how far every count strayed from its fair share. Small χ² = looks fair. Huge χ² = something is off.

Here we do that with digits: on each draw day, every digit 0–9 should fill about 10% of each position. χ² measures the total straying across all four positions.

The p-value answers: "if the lottery were perfectly fair, how often would luck alone produce a surprise score at least this big?" p = 0.80 means 80% of the time — totally ordinary. p below 0.05 (less than 1-in-20) is the usual threshold for raising an eyebrow.

One caution: we test 4 draw days, so even a fair lottery has roughly a 1-in-5 chance that some day dips below 0.05 by luck. Only a deviation that persists year after year would mean anything.

Model picks for the next draw

How does the Naive Bayes model pick numbers? (plain English)

Imagine four scoreboards, one for each digit position of a 4D number. Every time a winning number is drawn, each of its digits scores a point on its position's board. After thousands of draws, each board shows how often 0–9 has appeared in that position.

Example: if digit 9 appeared in the first position in 10.5% of past Wednesday numbers (a fair share would be exactly 10%), the model thinks "9 is slightly hot in position 1 on Wednesdays".

To score a full number like 9598, the model just multiplies its four digit shares together: (share of 9 in pos 1) × (share of 5 in pos 2) × (share of 9 in pos 3) × (share of 8 in pos 4). Highest products become the picks.

"Recency-weighted, 2-year half-life" simply means old draws fade: last month's draw counts almost fully, a draw from 2 years ago counts half, 4 years ago a quarter, and so on — so the scoreboards lean toward recent history.

How is the Markov chain different?

The Naive Bayes model judges each position on its own — it never notices which digits appear next to each other. The Markov chain looks exactly at that: it learns which digit tends to follow which.

Example: it might learn "after a 6, an 8 has followed slightly more often than 1-in-10". A number like 6818 then scores well because each step (6→8, 8→1, 1→8) has been a common hop in past winners.

Its score for a number = (how often the first digit starts a number) × (chance of the 2nd digit given the 1st) × (chance of the 3rd given the 2nd) × (chance of the 4th given the 3rd). Same recency fading as the other model.

What does "+20% vs uniform" actually mean?

"Uniform" is the fair-lottery baseline: every one of the 10,000 numbers has a 1-in-10,000 chance = 0.01%. A pick showing +20% means the model's estimate is 0.012% instead of 0.010%.

In betting terms: instead of 1-in-10,000, the model believes that number is roughly 1-in-8,300. Even if the model were right (the backtest shows it isn't), that difference would be far too small to overcome the payout odds — RM1 still returns about RM0.64 on average.

These small "edges" appear because past digit counts are never perfectly even — the same way 100 coin flips rarely land exactly 50/50. The unevenness is luck, not pattern.

Does the model actually work? (walk-forward backtest)

Replays the last 200 draws: for each one, the model is trained only on earlier draws, predicts its top-23 numbers, and we count how many real winning numbers it hit — compared against what 23 random picks would be expected to hit.

How does the backtest work, and why "9.3 expected if random"?

Think of it as a time machine test. We rewind to each of the last 200 draws and ask every model: "using only what was known before this draw, pick your best 23 numbers." Then we open that draw's real results and count how many of the 23 winning numbers the model caught. No peeking at the future is possible.

Why 9.3? Each draw publishes 23 winning numbers out of 10,000 possibilities. If you picked 23 numbers completely at random, your chance of catching each winner is 23÷10,000. Over 200 draws that adds up to 200 × 23 × 23 ÷ 10,000 ≈ 9–10 catches by pure luck. That's the bar: a model only "works" if it beats that number consistently, not just once.

About this app

Malaysia 4D Insights is an installable, offline-capable PWA for exploring results of the three national 4D operators: Magnum 4D, Da Ma Cai 1+3D and Sports Toto 4D.

Data pipeline

scrapers/ contains reference Python scrapers for the operators’ public result pages and ops/update-results.yml is a GitHub Actions template that refreshes data/draws.json after every draw. If a source only exposes the top prizes, the app keeps working — every statistic has a “Top 3 prizes only” mode.

Honesty box

A fair 4D draw is uniform and independent: every number has probability 1/10,000 of taking first prize, every draw, regardless of history. The statistics here describe the past; the χ² page tests (and with real data, generally confirms) that past frequencies carry no predictive power. Nothing in this app improves your odds of winning.
